Phase 1: The "Day One Student" (The Basics)
This phase builds rapport. The AI acts like a highly interested student trying to decide if they should buy the course.

The Core Subject: "So, to start things off simply—what exactly is this course about?"

The Tutor's Motivation: "There are a lot of topics out there. Why did you choose to create and teach a course on this specific subject?"

The Target Audience: "Who is the perfect student for this course? And just as importantly, who is this course not for?"

The Ultimate Outcome: "If a student enrolls today and puts in the work, what is the ultimate outcome? What will they be able to do by the end of the course?"

Phase 2: The Course Architecture (The Blueprint)
Once the high-level pitch is done, the AI needs to extract the structural data (the syllabus).

The High-Level Modules: "Can you walk me through the main modules or phases of this course?"

The Specific Topics: "Within those modules, what are the specific, key topics we are going to cover?"

The Format: "How is the learning structured? Are we focusing mostly on theory first, or do we jump straight into hands-on projects?"

Phase 3: Deep Extraction (The Digital Twin Fuel)
Now that the AI has the syllabus and the pitch, it pivots to capturing the tutor's unique teaching style, problem-solving methods, and edge cases. This is what makes the Digital Twin sound like the actual tutor.

The Friction Point: "When teaching this in the past, where do students most frequently get stuck or frustrated? How do you usually help them past that?"

The Core Framework: "What is your personal approach to teaching the hardest topic in this course? How do you break it down for a beginner?"

The Evaluation: "How do you test if a student has actually mastered these concepts, rather than just memorizing the steps you showed them?"

The Anti-Patterns: "What are the common bad habits or mistakes you see beginners fall into, and how do you correct them?"

Phase 4: Dynamic Follow-Up Logic
To make the AI feel like a real journalist (and not just a static form), program it to ask these quick follow-ups based on the tutor's answers in Phases 2 and 3:

If the tutor lists too many topics at once: "That is a great list. Out of all those topics, which one do you think is the absolute most critical for the student's success?"

If the tutor gives a vague outcome: "That sounds great in theory. Could you give me an example of a specific project or task they will be able to complete?"

Phase 1: The Core Data Payload (Metadata)
Before the AI asks open-ended questions, it needs to lock down the foundational schema. You can collect this via a quick form or introductory AI prompts.

Tutor Identity: Full name, current role/title, years of active industry experience, and highest credential or notable achievement.

Course Architecture: Exact course title, target audience (absolute beginner vs. upskilling professional), prerequisites, and estimated completion time.

The "North Star" Outcome: A single sentence defining exactly what the student will be able to build, solve, or execute by the end of the course.

Phase 2: The "Student Perspective" Intake
These questions mimic a curious, slightly hesitant student. They serve two purposes: they make the tutor comfortable by answering familiar questions, and they generate the foundational copy you need for the course landing page and initial Digital Twin interactions.

The Elevator Pitch: "If I have zero background in this field, how would you explain what this course is actually about in plain English?"

The 'Why Now': "Why is this specific skill critical right now? What real-world problem will this help me solve?"

The Reality Check: "What is the biggest misconception students have before they start learning this topic?"

The Success Metric: "What does a successful student look like after day one? What about after week four?"

Phase 3: The Digital Twin Deep Extraction
This is where you capture the tutor's unique teaching style, edge cases, and problem-solving frameworks. This is the fuel your agentic workflow needs to actually sound and teach like the tutor.

The Friction Point: "Where do students most frequently get stuck or frustrated in this course? Walk me through exactly how you unblock them."

The Framework: "What is your personal 'First Principles' approach to teaching this? How do you break down the most complex module?"

The Edge Case: "Can you give me an example of a real-world edge case or challenge related to this topic that standard textbooks completely miss?"

The Evaluation Mechanism: "How do you personally test if a student has actually mastered a concept, rather than just memorizing the steps?"

The Anti-Patterns: "What are the common bad habits or 'gotchas' you see beginners fall into, and how do you correct them early?"

Phase 4: AI Follow-Up Logic
To make the AI journalist feel real (and to get better data), you need to program dynamic follow-ups based on the tutor's response length and complexity.

If the tutor is too vague: Trigger: "That makes sense in theory, but could you walk me through a specific example or scenario where that happened?"

If the tutor is too technical: Trigger: "That's a great technical breakdown. If you had to explain that same concept to a freshman on day one, how would you simplify it?"

If the tutor mentions a framework: Trigger: "You mentioned [Framework Name]. What is the first step you take when applying that?
